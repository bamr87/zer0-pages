# Stakeholder Capitalism

## Definition

Stakeholder capitalism is a governance philosophy in which a company is run for the benefit of all its stakeholders — employees, customers, and affected communities — **alongside** shareholders, rather than for shareholder returns alone. In [[wiki/sources/wizard-topples-capitalist-dominance|the wizard post]] it is the second reform pillar, "stakeholder-first governance," described as "restructuring corporate boards to include employee and community representatives alongside shareholders."

## How It Works

- **Board composition** — seat employee and community representatives on the board, not just shareholder proxies.
- **Named accountability** — the practitioner's checklist requires "stakeholder representatives (employees, customers, affected communities)" to be "listed in the governance README."
- **Measured outcomes** — stakeholder welfare is scored, not just asserted, via [[wiki/concepts/impact-weighted-accounting|impact-weighted accounting]]'s social weights (living-wage compliance, workforce diversity, workplace safety).
- **Real-world scaffolding** — [[wiki/entities/b-corp|B Corp certification]] and the Embankment Project for Inclusive Capitalism (EPIC) as existing frameworks that formalize the philosophy.

## Why It Matters

Stakeholder capitalism is the post's answer to the "shareholder value vs. ethics" dichotomy: it reframes the two as compatible over long horizons. The post claims long-run shareholder returns are "*positively* correlated with stakeholder welfare," and that "the contradiction only appears under quarterly-earnings tunnel vision." In other words, shareholder primacy isn't refuted by ethics — it's refuted by a longer time horizon.

## Examples

- **Governance README** — a repo whose README names the community and employee reps on its board is doing the checklist's stakeholder-representation item literally.
- **B Corp as proof** — certification evidence that firms can meet verified social/environmental standards while remaining for-profit.
